A Genealogy of a Rule of Silence: Origin, Function, and Unequal Transgression

An implicit norm governs one's relationship to money: not revealing one's economic situation. This norm applies in both directions — concealing wealth as much as poverty — but it sanctions these two transgressions in a radically unequal way. This text traces the origin of this rule, questions its function, and introduces the categories that allow it to be named precisely.

Four ideas, one at a time

1. The same norm, two directions, two regimes of sanction

The modesty that forbids flaunting one's wealth and the shame that forbids revealing one's poverty proceed from one and the same injunction: to keep silent about one's economic position. But the asymmetry of treatment is immediate. Whoever exposes their wealth is judged clumsy, sometimes arrogant — a judgment of manners. Whoever exposes their poverty is judged suspect, sometimes calculating — a judgment that directly touches their credibility as a speaking subject. In the vocabulary of the normative theatre, this norm functions as an implicit scene that everyone is supposed to play without ever discussing its rules: whoever refuses to enter it exposes, through this very refusal, the scene itself.

2. A norm with no text, but with very real consequences

No contract formally states the prohibition on revealing one's salary. And yet transgressing it produces an immediate social cost — embarrassment, judgment, loss of credibility. This is precisely what orthonormation designates: the state of conformity to a local norm that never needs to be written down to produce very concrete sanctioning effects on whoever departs from it.

3. The genealogical question: origin of the norm, beneficiary of its maintenance

This norm was clearly not instituted by the very person it constrains most severely to silence. A rule that sanctions transgression more heavily on the side of poverty than on the side of wealth cannot claim neutrality in its origin. Its structural function is to serve those for whom the visibility of inequalities would constitute a discomfort — the maintenance of silence benefits first and foremost those who have no interest in seeing the disproportion exposed.

4. Two vocabularies for naming this same mechanism

Pierre Bourdieu proposes the notion of symbolic violence: a domination that operates not through direct constraint, but through the internalization, within the dominated person themselves, of the categories that justify their own position — the shame of poverty experienced as a natural fact rather than as a social construction. Without necessarily subscribing to the whole of his theoretical framework, this precise mechanism overlaps with a category developed independently in this work: interpersonal policing — the diffuse apparatus, with no identifiable center or leader, by which a group enforces a local norm by sanctioning whoever departs from it, not out of deliberate malice but as a reflex to maintain the shared scene.

What we want to point out, in three sentences

1. The same norm of silence about money exists for wealth and for poverty, but it is never sanctioned with the same severity depending on who transgresses it.

2. This norm was not instituted by those it punishes most harshly — it structurally benefits those who profit from the silence maintained on inequalities.

3. Transgressing this norm by revealing one's poverty is therefore less a breach of propriety than the exercise of an existential sovereignty: the refusal to let a norm whose origin and function are questionable dictate what can or cannot be said about oneself.
