The Absence of Shame: When Dignity Protects Despite the Harm

Going through a real blow to one's material conditions of existence requires neither heroic pride nor resigned shame. This text explains how a person can refuse to let their economic situation define them, without claiming any pride on either side of that situation.

Four ideas, one at a time

1. Two dignities, not one

There is a difference between ontological dignity — the inherent worth of a person by the simple fact of existing, which depends neither on position nor salary — and existential dignity — the concrete conditions that allow one to continue existing as a dignified subject. This second dignity is not limited to housing or material resources: it equally includes the way one is treated in relation to others — contempt, insult, lack of consideration are attacks on existential dignity just as much as material precariousness. The first dignity, meanwhile, can be neither granted nor taken away by anyone.

2. What protects when one of the two is attacked

When existential dignity is attacked — loss of income, precariousness, contempt — ontological dignity can function as an existential cushioning: the certainty of one's own worth, independent of any outside validation, allows one to go through the blow without collapsing. This is not ignoring the real difficulty. It is refusing to let it become the sole verdict on one's worth.

3. Neither pride nor shame — a harder position to hold than either

There is no particular pride in being poor, any more than there would be in becoming rich — becoming rich would produce more relief at no longer having to fight than any kind of pride. What remains, in both cases, is the absence of shame: the refusal to let one's economic situation become a definition of self. This refusal is not, however, a comfort acquired once and for all — it is lived as a constant struggle between a sincere indifference to others' judgment and the need to regulate that indifference in public, so as not to be read as someone "who lacks self-awareness."

4. A capacity, not a superiority

This mechanism connects directly to the principle of existential sovereignty: the capacity to assess the legitimacy of an external norm before letting it dictate one's behavior, and to refuse to submit to it when that legitimacy is lacking. This distance implies no moral hierarchy toward those who live the same situation without achieving the same distance — it remains a capacity developed under particular circumstances, never proof of superior worth over others.

What we want to point out, in three sentences

1. Ontological dignity and existential dignity are not the same thing — the second can be attacked, in material conditions as much as in relations with others, without the first collapsing.

2. There is no pride either in poverty or in some hypothetical wealth — what remains is the absence of shame, a constant struggle between sincere indifference and its public regulation.

3. This capacity founds no moral superiority over those who live the same situation differently — it remains a capacity, not a judgment passed on others.
